Prospecting for ecommerce leads by hand tops out at a few sites an hour. You Google a
niche, eyeball a store, hunt for an email, and write a note from scratch, and most
leads die at the "who do I email" step. A small team simply cannot produce a steady
flow of qualified leads that way.

So we built an agent that does it. It discovers candidate ecommerce sites, scans
their public pages for growth-marketing problems, estimates how big each store is,
finds and verifies a contact, scores the lead with reasons you can read, and drafts a
personalized outreach message. A human reviews and sends. The team spends its time on
conversations, not prospecting.

## What it looks like

The agent scans a weak prospect store, logs each detected issue, sizes the store and
finds a verified owner, then turns the scan into a scored lead with a drafted pitch.

## Who it is for

The agent has two people around it. A growth operator configures what a good lead
looks like and reviews the ranked queue. A sales partner sends the approved message
and reports how it went. The prospect merchant is the target, but never interacts
with the system: the agent only reads their public pages.

## How it works, in one pipeline

The agent runs a staged pipeline. It discovers candidate sites, scans each one for
growth-marketing problems, estimates the store's size, finds and verifies a contact,
scores the lead across how well it fits, how reachable it is, and how fixable its
problems are, and drafts a message that leads with the specific insight. The result
lands in a ranked queue for a human to review.

The design idea that makes this affordable at volume is cheap-first: the agent spends
free signals on every candidate and only pays for the expensive checks once a lead has
earned them by clearing the fit gate. Spend tracks lead quality, so the cost per lead
stays under control.

## Why a human still sends every message

The agent drafts; a human approves and sends. That is a deliberate choice, not a
limitation. Trust, email deliverability, and the law all argue for a person on the
send: a merchant should get relevant outreach about real problems on their site, not
be spammed. So every message is reviewed before it goes out, the agent only ever reads
public pages and respects a site's crawl rules, and personal contact data is kept in a
governed store with a lawful basis recorded and a working opt-out on every message.

<Callout kind="note" title="Detect, not change">
In this phase the agent only finds what is wrong on a prospect's site. It never
changes their site. Detecting a fixable problem is the point; fixing it is a separate
service the agent's scanning intelligence is the foundation for.
</Callout>

## The bigger picture

Finding what is wrong on a store is the same skill as fixing it. This engine is the
front door to a larger idea: a service that will eventually auto-remediate the very
problems this agent detects. Building lead generation first is deliberate. It earns
the right to auto-heal sites by first proving we can reliably find what is wrong, at
scale, cheaply, and accurately enough to put in front of a prospect. Every scan
teaches the engine a little more about what a real growth-marketing problem looks
like, which is exactly what a self-healing agent will need to know.
