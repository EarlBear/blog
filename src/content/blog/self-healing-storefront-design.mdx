Continuous, statistically sound conversion optimization is the highest-leverage
growth lever an online store has, and it is precisely the one a do-it-yourself
merchant cannot pull. This is the high-level design for the agent that pulls it for
them: it turns detected problems and store analytics into a ranked experiment
backlog, generates on-brand variants, runs them safely on live traffic, ships the
winners, and reports the dollar lift in plain language.

This is the internal, as-is design. It is the fix half of the scan to heal loop:
where the site scanner finds what is wrong on a storefront, this agent resolves it
through continuous, measured experimentation.

## Who it is for

Three people act on this agent. A do-it-yourself store owner runs an ecommerce shop,
is not a marketing expert, and wants more revenue without hiring one or risking the
store. An operator (us) runs the agent as a managed service across many stores and
needs to supervise it safely and profitably. A shopper browses the storefront and
never touches the agent, but their behavior is the signal every experiment reads.

## Executive summary

The problem. Continuous, statistically sound conversion optimization is the
highest-leverage growth lever an online store has, and it is precisely the one a
do-it-yourself merchant cannot pull. Today's testing tools assume a marketing expert
who knows what to test, can build the variants, can interpret the statistics, and is
willing to risk live sales. Most small and mid-sized merchants therefore do nothing,
or guess and ship changes with no control group, leaving conversion and revenue on
the table.

The solution. A self-healing storefront agent turns the conversion problems already
detected by the site scanner, plus the store's own analytics, into a ranked backlog
of experiments; generates on-brand variants; runs them safely on live traffic with
statistics suited to the store's traffic level; ships the winners; and reports the
dollar lift in plain language. It is delivered as a managed service: we (the
operators) supervise the agent across many stores, while the store owner sets the
rules and watches the results.

## Context: the experiment loop

The agent sits on a customer's live storefront. It consumes signals (the scanner's
detected CRO problems, the store's own analytics, the product catalog), runs a
continuous experiment loop, and produces shipped storefront changes plus a
plain-language wins ledger the owner can read. The do-it-yourself store owner sets
the rules and watches; the operator supervises across many stores.

The loop ideate, design, run, measure, decide, then iterate is the heart of the
agent. The scanner findings input is the bridge from the site scanner: its detected
problems seed this agent's hypothesis backlog, so there is no cold start.

service. "Experiment" and "test" are used interchangeably for a single A/B or A/B/n

## Why this is hard

Two problem domains drive the work: why do-it-yourself owners do not run experiments
today, and why automating experimentation safely is hard.

Owners do not test because they do not know what to test, do not have the traffic or
time to test properly, find the tools built for analysts rather than merchants, and
fear breaking a live revenue store. Automating it safely is hard because a naive
system that calls winners on thin data will confidently ship losers, mutating a live
revenue site puts money on the line every minute, generic ideation produces noise
rather than lift, and the entire value proposition rests on attributable revenue the
owner believes.

### The statistical reality for mid-market stores

Because target stores span low to mid traffic, the classic fixed-sample frequentist
approach most tools default to is often a poor fit:

- A store with about 500 weekly users at a 2% baseline conversion needs on the order
  of 16 weeks to detect even a large (50%) relative improvement with fixed-sample
  frequentist methods.[^a3] Most owners will not wait, so tests die inconclusive.
- Sequential and Bayesian methods adapt during the test and can require 20 to 80%
  fewer users than an equivalent fixed-sample test,[^a4] and they yield an intuitive
  output ("91% chance B beats A") instead of a p-value.[^a2]
- CRO best practice for smaller stores is to test bold, high-impact changes (at
  least a 30% expected relative lift) rather than micro-tweaks like button color,
  because only large effects are detectable on thin traffic.[^a1]

<Assumption>Target stores are predominantly Shopify or Shopify-like; the design
should not hard-assume Shopify, but Shopify is the dominant case to optimize
for.</Assumption> This shapes the execution-model options below.

## Proposed design

The target state is an autonomous experimentation agent wrapped in an autonomy and
guardrail engine, operating a continuous loop on each customer store. The agent's
reasoning (ideate, design, decide) is independent of the serving mechanism (how a
variant is actually shown to traffic); that separation is what lets the serving
approach in D1 change without rewriting the agent.

The execution layer is deliberately a single abstracted box. The three architecture
options below are three ways to fill that box; the rest of the agent is unchanged
across them.

### Architecture options

All three share the same agent brain, guardrail engine, and data model. They differ
only in how variants are served to live traffic, which drives platform coupling,
speed to value, flicker risk, and how much we build versus integrate.

The recommendation is a phased path: start Shopify-native (option 3), converge on
self-hosted serving (option 2). Phase A ships as a Shopify-native app for the fastest
onboarding, native distribution, and least flicker risk, letting us validate the
agent before investing in our own serving infrastructure. Even in Phase A we run our
own Bayesian/sequential decisioning on the raw events so the measurement engine is
ours from day one. Phase B builds self-hosted edge/JS serving once the agent is
proven, removing per-store platform fees and unifying integration across all stores.
The execution-model abstraction means this swap does not touch the agent brain.

This phasing mirrors the scanner's philosophy: earn the right to build the harder,
higher-control system by first proving the cheap version works.

## Components

The agent decomposes into eight components. The first five are the agent brain
(serving-independent); the last three are the control, execution, and surface
layers.

<FlowDiagram
  title="Component decomposition (C1 to C8)"
  desc="The serving-independent brain (C1 to C5) plus the guardrail, execution-adapter, and surface layers."
  mermaid={`flowchart LR
    C1[C1 Ideation] --> C2[C2 Variant gen]
    C2 --> C3[C3 Orchestrator]
    C3 --> C4[C4 Measurement]
    C4 --> C5[C5 Ledger]
    C4 -.iterate.-> C1
    C3 --> C6[C6 Guardrail]
    C6 --> C7[C7 Adapter]
    C7 --> C4
    C6 -.rollback.-> C7
    C5 --> C8[C8 Surfaces]

/>

- C1 Ideation engine (D4): turns scanner findings, store analytics, and CRO best
  practice into a prioritized hypothesis backlog.
- C2 Variant generator (D5): produces concrete, on-brand, accessible copy and layout
  variants for a hypothesis.
- C3 Experiment orchestrator: configures, launches, and lifecycle-manages an
  experiment against the execution layer.
- C4 Measurement engine (D2): chooses the statistical method per store traffic and
  computes the running decision (ship, kill, continue).
- C5 Attribution and ledger (D6): computes per-experiment and cumulative conversion
  and revenue lift; renders the owner-facing wins ledger.
- C6 Guardrail and autonomy engine (D3): enforces autonomy tier per category,
  blast-radius caps, revenue floors; runs real-time health monitoring and
  auto-rollback.
- C7 Execution adapter (D1): abstracts the serving mechanism behind one interface.
- C8 Surfaces: owner dashboard (guardrails, approvals, ledger) and operator console
  (supervision, tuning, override).

Two cross-cutting flows run underneath. The real-time safety loop: while any

## Autonomy and hard guardrails

Autonomy is gated by risk and configured per category. Low-risk, easily reversible
categories (copy/content and layout/UX) can run fully autonomously within guardrails.
Higher-risk categories (pricing/offers, checkout/funnel) are supported but require
human approval: the agent ideates and designs the experiment, but a human ships it.
The model mirrors a Claude-Code-style permission prompt.

<Callout kind="warning" title="Hard guardrails: inviolable at every tier">
The agent must never: ship a change that breaches the owner's revenue floor or
blast-radius cap; leave a harmful experiment running (it must auto-rollback on a
health breach within the monitoring window); autonomously change pricing or checkout
(those always require human approval); generate variants that violate the brand/voice
profile or accessibility standards; lose the audit trail (every action is logged and
attributable); touch a customer's no-touch zones; or exceed its least-privilege
scopes (no PII, no payment data).
</Callout>

## Open questions

The architecture, components, and guardrails are settled. The main open items:

- The scanner to agent interface: shared database, event stream, or API call?
- Does every store get a permanent portfolio holdback (cleanest attribution, small
  lift cost), or only during onboarding?
- What are the default guardrail values (revenue floor, blast-radius cap) shipped
  before an owner customizes them?
- Where is the "too small to test" traffic floor, below which the agent declines to
  run an experiment because no method can decide in a reasonable window?
- The concrete context-management strategy across the agent phases, so scanner
  findings flow to ideation to generation to measurement without losing context or
  exceeding LLM limits.
- Pricing and packaging of the managed service (per-store flat, percentage of
  attributed lift, tiered): a commercial, not architectural, question.

## North star: self-healing beyond CRO

This design fixes one thing well: conversion. But an agent that can safely observe a
live storefront, form a hypothesis, run a controlled experiment, and ship or roll
back the result is a foundation, and a foundation opens more than one door. The
scanner already detects problems well beyond CRO.
