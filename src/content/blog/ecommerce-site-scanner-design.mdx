A two-person founding team, a technical growth operator and a sales partner, cannot
manually produce a steady flow of qualified ecommerce leads. Today they prospect by
hand: Googling niches, eyeballing sites, hunting for an email, and writing each note
from scratch. This tops out at a few sites an hour, gives no consistent score, loses
most leads at the "who do I email" step, and leaves no audit trail.

This is the internal, as-is design for the engine that replaces that. An
LLM agent drives a staged, cheap-first pipeline that discovers candidate sites, scans
their public pages for growth-marketing problems, estimates size, finds and verifies
a contact, scores the lead with explainable reasons, and drafts a personalized
outreach message for a human to send.

<Callout kind="note" title="Scope">
This document covers Phase 1: scan, score, outreach for lead generation. The agent
is the front door to a larger agentic service for self-healing growth-marketing
sites (technical/perf, SEO, CRO, and content auto-remediation). Self-healing is the
north-star vision that motivates this work but is explicitly out of scope for this
design; Phase 1 builds and validates detection, and the self-healing service fixes
sites later.
</Callout>

## Executive summary

A two-person team cannot manually produce a steady, high-quality flow of qualified
ecommerce leads. The manual baseline tops out at a handful of sites per hour,
produces no consistent score, loses most leads at the contact-finding step, and
leaves no audit trail. The data needed to do this well, which sites to scan, how big
they are, and who to email, is fragmented across paid providers of uneven quality,
with real legal constraints on collection and outreach.

The proposal is an autonomous scanning and lead engine: an LLM agent drives a staged,
cheap-first pipeline that discovers candidate sites, scans their public pages for
growth-marketing problems, estimates size, finds and verifies a contact, scores the
lead with explainable reasons, and drafts a personalized outreach message for a human
to send. The same scanning intelligence is the foundation for the future self-healing
service: Phase 1 builds and validates detection.

The most consequential choices are the "how do we get the data" decisions: site
discovery (D1), size and traffic estimation (D2), and contact discovery (D3), all
currently leaning toward layered, cheap-first pipelines; the build-versus-buy
recommendation is a purpose-built engine over best-of-breed APIs. Outreach stays
draft-only with a human on every send (D6, decided).

Expected value: <Assumption>50 to 100 qualified, contactable leads per
week</Assumption> at a controlled cost per lead, plus a reusable signal taxonomy that
de-risks Phase 2. The biggest open questions are the exact ICP, the per-scan page
budget, the cost-per-lead ceiling, and the EU-versus-US outreach posture.

## Why this is hard

The work is driven by getting the data (the hard part) and turning it into action.
No scalable way to find which sites to scan; no reliable signal for how big a site
is; no reliable way to find who to email (the step where most leads die); no
consistent score; and generic outreach that gets ignored. The gaps compound: weak
discovery feeds weak qualification feeds weak contact feeds generic outreach, so
point-fixing one stage does not move the outcome. The design must address the
pipeline end to end.

## Proposed design

The engine is a staged pipeline driven by an LLM agent, where each stage is
cheap-first and degrades gracefully: it spends free or low-cost signals on every
candidate and escalates to paid providers only as a lead earns it. The pipeline
produces a scored, contactable, insight-rich lead with a drafted outreach message for
human review.

<FlowDiagram
  title="The cheap-first pipeline"
  desc="Operator config drives the discover, scan, size, contact, score, draft pipeline into a lead queue the operator reviews and the partner sends."
  mermaid={`flowchart LR
    ICP[ICP config] --> DISC(Discover)
    DISC -->|domains| SCAN(Scan)
    SCAN -->|signals| SIZE(Size)
    SIZE -->|tier| CONTACT(Contact)
    CONTACT -->|verified| SCORE(Score)

    DRAFT --> QUEUE[(Lead queue)]
    QUEUE --> OP[Operator]
    OP -->|approve| BD[Partner]`}

Five principles carry through every stage: cheap-first, escalate on merit; confidence,
not certainty; PII-free discovery with governed contact; every scan is a reusable
asset; and a human on the send.

### Architecture options

The build-versus-buy choice is really a question of who it serves. Each actor's use
cases above set a bar: the operator needs a ranked queue with *auditable* reasons
(not a vendor's opaque score), the agent needs to *own the signal taxonomy* so a scan
is a reusable asset for the Phase 2 fix list, and EarlBear needs cost-per-lead under
control at volume. The three options differ on how much of that they let us own — from
renting a generic stack to owning a purpose-built engine. The criteria below are
scored against those needs, not just engineering effort.

Option 2 wins because it is the only one that clears every actor's bar at once. It
gives the operator auditable reasons and the agent an owned signal taxonomy — which
option 1 (wrapping a generic orchestrator) cannot, because the score and the taxonomy
live inside the vendor. It controls cost-per-lead at volume without option 3's
premature engineering load, which a two-person team can't carry. The on-ramp borrows
option 1's speed: Phase 1a stands up the pipeline skeleton but leans on the
highest-leverage paid API per stage to prove the loop and unit economics fast; Phase
1b deepens the scanner and the signal taxonomy and tunes the cheap-first escalation to
the cost-per-lead ceiling. Option 3 (maximal in-house over raw substrate) is deferred
until volume economics justify the engineering. Every external provider sits behind
our own interface, so any single vendor is swappable without re-architecting.

## Components

Each component is one pipeline stage plus the cross-cutting agent, store, and config.
Every external provider sits behind the component's interface so it is swappable.

The scanner emits the structured signal taxonomy per site; the size estimator returns
an ordinal tier with confidence; the contact finder runs on-site to waterfall to
verify; the scorer computes an explainable score across fit, reachability, and
fixability with reasons; and the compliance gate enforces the crawl and outreach
guardrails and manages the suppression list.

## Compliance guardrails

Compliance is a first-class constraint, not an afterthought.

<Callout kind="warning" title="The hard guardrails">
Read-only logged-out public pages only; honor robots.txt and rate limits; never
bypass anti-bot or CAPTCHA; keep discovery PII-free and isolate contact PII in a
governed store with retention limits and a lawful basis and source recorded; every
outreach carries sender identity, a physical address, and a working opt-out honored
within two days; SPF, DKIM, and DMARC plus one-click unsubscribe on the sending
domain; and a maintained suppression list.
</Callout>

## Open questions

The biggest open items: the exact ICP (platforms, categories, geos, size band); the
concrete page budget per scan; the cost-per-lead ceiling; which CRM and email
channel; the concrete D7 stack; the Phase 1a starting provider per stage; what
outcome signals feed the learning loop and how they adjust scoring; and whether Phase
1 outreach is EU-inclusive or US-only, which changes the legal bar.

## The north star it seeds

This engine is the front door to a larger agentic service for self-healing
growth-marketing sites. Where this agent finds what is wrong on a prospect's site,
the future service fixes it. Building lead generation first earns the right to
auto-heal sites: we prove we can reliably find what is wrong, at scale, cheaply, and
accurately enough to put in front of a prospect, before we are trusted to change
anything. Every scan in Phase 1 accrues toward the signal taxonomy that becomes the
action space for the self-healing agent.
